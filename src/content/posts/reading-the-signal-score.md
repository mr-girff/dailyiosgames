**TL;DR** — Signal measures *momentum and traction*, not *quality*. A high signal tells you a game is moving right now; it does not tell you the game is good, fair, or worth your money. Pair signal with the verdict, the rating count, and the red-flag list before you install anything.

## What signal actually captures

Signal is a composite of the things that indicate a title is getting attention: chart movement, update cadence, rating velocity, and how recently it launched. A fresh game climbing the charts with a burst of new ratings will score high. A beloved evergreen that hasn't moved in months will score low even if it's excellent.

In other words: **signal is a "what's happening" number, not a "what's good" number.** Treat it like a trending indicator, not a review.

## Why a 20 isn't a green light

The maximum signal in our dataset shows up on titles that are, frankly, all over the map — some are genuinely great, and some are aggressive live-service machines buying their way up the charts. A 20 next to a casino or hyper-monetized match-3 title usually means "lots of money and marketing are pushing this right now," which is the *opposite* of a recommendation for most players.

This is exactly why we don't rank by signal alone. Signal answers "is this moving?" The verdict answers "should you care?"

## The verdict layer

On top of signal we attach an editorial verdict to titles we've looked at closely:

- **build** — worth your time; the loop is fair and the game respects you.
- **watch** — interesting but unproven, or carrying a caveat worth monitoring.
- **skip** — high signal, low reason to install (usually a monetization or dark-pattern problem).

Most titles carry no verdict at all, which simply means we haven't editorialized them yet — not that they're bad. When a verdict *is* present, it should outweigh the raw signal number every time.

## How to actually use these numbers

1. **Start with the verdict.** A `build` beats a high signal with no verdict.
2. **Check rating count, not just rating.** A 4.9 from 40 ratings is noise; a 4.7 from 400,000 is a real signal.
3. **Read the red flags.** We surface predatory-timer, ad-overload, and pay-to-progress patterns. One flag is a shrug; three is a skip.
4. **Use signal last** — as a tiebreaker between two games you already like.
